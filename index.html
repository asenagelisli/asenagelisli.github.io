Smoking Kills!

A visual interpretation to smoking and cancer

Made by Denizhan Kara

Tobacco use is responsible for 480,000 premature deaths each year and almost $300 billion in health care costs. A new study from the American Cancer Society reveals that smoking is responsible for about one-third of all cancer deaths in the nation overall. Most deaths from smoking occur in older populations. When we look at the breakdown of deaths from smoking by age, we see that it is mainly older populations that are affected.Note that the share of lower ages increase slightly as the smoking gets more and more popular among the younger population over the years.

Finally, to show the correlation between smoking and cancer in various countries, Global Burden of Disease published a detailed report in late 20th century. The data provides the number of smoke sales per adult per day versus the lung cancer mortalities in late 20th century. In many countries we see a significant rise, peak and then decline in lung cancer death rates ,which are driven by the trends in smoking. Browse the different country statistics using the dropdown menu and explore the correlation by mousing over.

Observe that lung cancer trends follow the smoking trends with a certain lag. Note that the decrease in smoking trends are generally more significant in richer countries due to regulations, which are followed by even sharper decreases in the lung cancer mortalities.

More on smoking and other risks ...

Smoking causes cancer, heart disease, stroke, lung diseases, diabetes, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), which includes emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Apart from direct smoking induced mortalities, the secondary health issues collectively cause more fatalities than drugs.
